Charley, a young greenhorn from Germany who becomes the narrator of this adventure trilogy, sets out to see the world. He travels to America and, upon arriving in St. Louis, although not lacking in material wealth, he can only work as a private tutor. Fortunately, with the help of a gunsmith named Mr. Henry, he fulfills his dream of visiting the Wild West by working as a surveyor for a railroad company building tracks across the heart of the United States. Under the guidance of Sam Hawkens, Charley gradually learns how to become a "westman." However, his experiences pale in comparison to the day he meets Winnetou, the son of an Apache tribal chief. From Winnetou, Charley—a young man from the modern world—learns from someone labeled as a "wild" Indian. Charley, who later earns the title Old Shatterhand, also comes to realize that the railroad being constructed had not yet received permission from its rightful landowners, the Apache people. This is the seminal novel by Karl May (1842–1912), the first book in a trilogy that established him as one of the world’s leading travel adventure writers. The book also sheds light on the extinction of American fauna like bison and mustangs and, importantly, the protagonist's struggle to defend the rights of Native Americans facing near extinction. This humanitarian novel conveys ideas about friendship between races, tribes, and religions. Environmental activists and advocates for the rights of the oppressed might find this book especially valuable.

Like all of Hermann Hesse's major works, Demian, written by the then 40-year-old author in the midst of World War I, has an equally unusual and fascinating history of its creation and reception. This book, completed in the fall of 1917, was not published until June 1919, half a year after the end of the war, due to the author's anonymity. Hesse had submitted the manuscript to the publisher as the debut work of a young, ailing poet named Emil Sinclair, a socially critical writer who had so far only gained attention in newspapers and magazines through pacifist appeals and stories (which were also written by Hesse). Despite this pseudonymous disguise, the book received an overwhelmingly enthusiastic reception and was awarded the Fontane Prize for the best debut work by an emerging author in the same year it was published. Thomas Mann compared the book's electrifying impact to that of Goethe's Werther, noting that it "struck the nerve of the time with uncanny precision and enraptured an entire youth, who believed that a herald of their deepest life had emerged from among them." By the time the pseudonym was revealed in May 1920, three editions had been published. Under Hesse's real name, an additional 93 editions followed during his lifetime
